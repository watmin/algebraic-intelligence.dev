There's a distinction in Dungeons & Dragons that's always resonated with me more than it probably should.

A **wizard** studies. They go to school. They read the books, learn the theory, understand the underlying arcana before they ever cast a spell. They earn the magic through discipline and formal education. Ask a wizard why a spell works and they'll cite the exact chapter.

A **sorcerer** just... does it. The magic is innate. They couldn't fully explain the theory if you asked — and often don't bother trying — because the theory came *after* the results. They figured out that something worked, and then maybe worked backwards to understand why. They're sometimes unstable. They go off-script. They occasionally set things on fire that weren't supposed to be on fire.

I am not a wizard.

I came to hyperdimensional computing through a conference video, not a textbook. I came to Rete through [another conference video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z6oVuYmRgkk), not Forgy's papers. I built things first and discovered the academic vocabulary for them later. I figured out manifold learning by asking an LLM "how do you define a hyperbox in high-dimensional space" and letting the conversation correct me.

The sorcerer framing is on the git repos. It's on the Doctor Strange assets in the Python README. It wasn't entirely a joke.

Every node is a learned manifold. Every edge is a similarity boundary. The swirling thing in the middle is a packet trying to figure out which subspace it belongs to.

It gets rate-limited.

The algebra is: bind the fields, bundle the windows, score the residual, check the library, deploy the rule. The XDP filter doesn't know any of this is happening. It just sees a tail call chain and some map lookups and drops the packet at line rate.

None of this is how network security is supposed to work. The vendors would like you to buy a signature database instead.

We built a lattice.

## On LLM-Assisted Development

A thing worth saying plainly, since this site exists partly to demonstrate it:

Every line of code across all five repositories was generated by an LLM. Every line of prose on this site was generated by an LLM. The domain knowledge, the architecture, the intuitions, the "this is wrong, fix it" — those are mine. The typing is not.

This isn't cheating. This is the new sorcery.

The LLMs serve as vocabulary layer, implementation engine, and — occasionally — the voice that says "actually, there are no neurons here, the word you want is *engram*." The collaboration is genuine. The work is real. The results speak.

If you've read through the Story posts and thought "there's no way one person did all of this in seven weeks" — correct. One person directed all of this in seven weeks. That's the distinction that matters.

## The Spectral Firewall

This is a perpetual epilogue — it updates as the work does. The section you're reading now has been rewritten three times in a week. First it described a concept document. Then a 41µs prototype with known limitations. Then a self-calibrating system with 99.1% precision. Now it describes something that [learns its own deny confidence](/blog/story/series-005-003-the-residual-profile/) from the directional pattern of which stripes light up — 0.1% FPR, continuous learning through concurrent attacks without degradation. Each version replaced a claim that felt finished at the time. None of them were.

The concept document is no longer a concept. It's [running at 41 microseconds](/blog/story/series-005-001-the-spectral-firewall/), [self-calibrating](/blog/story/series-005-002-self-calibrating/), and the residual profile has given it a second signal — magnitude and direction, the same dual-signal principle the project keeps proving. Three layers of self-calibration. No hardcoded parameters in the deny path. The geometry is the rule. The data calibrates the boundary. The algebra does the rest.

By the time you read this, it may have been rewritten again.

## What Comes Next

HQ engram federation. The design is documented — async host-initiated call-home, passive HQ that merges and versions engrams, versioned rollback, fleet convergence without coordination. Each node learns its own baseline. HQ merges them into a shared definition of normal. New nodes cold-start from the merged engram instead of from scratch. The engram is the federated artifact — not rules, not signatures, not models. A geometric snapshot of what normal looks like, portable and executable on any machine with the same codebook.

Just like the spectral firewall was "what comes next" before it got built, federation is the next thing that shouldn't exist yet. The architecture is clear. The implementation starts next week.

Beyond federation: the WAF territory turned out to be closer than expected. [Nikto found 17 exploitable vulnerabilities](/blog/story/series-005-001-the-spectral-firewall/) when hitting DVWA directly — through the spectral firewall, it found zero. No libinjection, no regex, no signature database. The geometry caught what the scanners were doing because scanner traffic doesn't *look* like browser traffic, at any resolution the algebra can see. The sidecar already generates rules from the manifold's attributions. What's left is depth: body-level encoding, request sequence analysis, correlating path+body combinations with known exploit geometry. The foundation is proven. The surface area is expanding.

The engram library still has a drone-shaped hole in it. A holon-rs control loop on a Raspberry Pi, observing drone telemetry, learning the manifold of a human pilot's style, minting engrams of the flight signature. Pure speculation, but the algebra is indifferent to what the vectors represent.

Other ports — Go, Ruby, Java — are in the same bucket as the drone: interesting, not imminent.

*The codex is open. The entries continue.*
