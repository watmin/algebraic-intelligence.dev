There's a distinction in Dungeons & Dragons that's always resonated with me more than it probably should.

A **wizard** studies. They go to school. They read the books, learn the theory, understand the underlying arcana before they ever cast a spell. They earn the magic through discipline and formal education. Ask a wizard why a spell works and they'll cite the exact chapter.

A **sorcerer** just... does it. The magic is innate. They couldn't fully explain the theory if you asked — and often don't bother trying — because the theory came *after* the results. They figured out that something worked, and then maybe worked backwards to understand why. They're sometimes unstable. They go off-script. They occasionally set things on fire that weren't supposed to be on fire.

I am not a wizard.

I came to hyperdimensional computing through a conference video, not a textbook. I came to Rete through [another conference video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Z6oVuYmRgkk), not Forgy's papers. I built things first and discovered the academic vocabulary for them later. I figured out manifold learning by asking an LLM "how do you define a hyperbox in high-dimensional space" and letting the conversation correct me.

The sorcerer framing is on the git repos. It's on the Doctor Strange assets in the Python README. It wasn't entirely a joke.

## The Spectral Firewall

The concept document is no longer a concept. It's [running at 41 microseconds](/blog/story/series-005-001-the-spectral-firewall/), [self-calibrating](/blog/story/series-005-002-self-calibrating/), and the residual profile has given it a second signal — magnitude and direction, the same dual-signal principle the project keeps proving. Three layers of self-calibration. No hardcoded parameters in the deny path. The geometry is the rule. The data calibrates the boundary. The algebra does the rest.

## The Thought Machine

The algebra pointed at markets. [Same encoding, same journal, same cosine](/blog/story/series-006-001-the-thought-machine/) — different thoughts. The visual encoder captured every pixel of a price chart and predicted nothing. The thought encoder — 120 named facts per candle, composed via bind/bundle — predicted 57%. Then 62% with a larger vocabulary.

[The conviction-accuracy curve](/blog/story/series-006-002-the-conviction-curve/) turned out to be exponential, continuous, and monotonic. One economic parameter — minimum acceptable edge — derives the operating point. The system identifies trend extremes with high confidence, flips the prediction, and catches reversals at 60% across six years of BTC.

The vocabulary is the model. The discriminant is learned. The curve evaluates. 84 atoms got 57%. 107 got 62%. The hyperspace has room for thousands. The question is no longer "can machines trade?" It's "what should machines think about?"

## What Comes Next

**Multi-expert architecture**: N thought journals running in parallel, each with a different vocabulary subset. The Ichimoku expert, the RSI momentum expert, the Wyckoff volume expert. Each develops its own discriminant. Each produces its own conviction-accuracy curve. The curves compete. Champions emerge from the geometry, not from human judgment about which school of trading is "right."

**Thought engrams**: the engram system from the DDoS lab, applied to market regime detection. When the discriminant's eigenvalue structure shifts, that's a regime transition. Mint an engram. On re-encounter, recall the discriminant that worked in that regime. The immune system metaphor, applied to markets.

**HQ engram federation**: async host-initiated call-home, passive HQ that merges and versions engrams, fleet convergence without coordination. The engram is the federated artifact — a geometric snapshot of what normal looks like, portable and executable on any machine with the same codebook.

**Cross-asset generalization**: same architecture, different market, one economic parameter. If the conviction-accuracy curve is steep on a new asset with the same vocabulary, the system works there too. If it's flat, that asset needs different thoughts.

The drone-shaped hole in the engram library hasn't gone away. Neither have the language ports. The surface area keeps expanding.
